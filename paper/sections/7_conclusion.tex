\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a consolidated empirical and computational evaluation of the Discrete Symplectic Cosmology (DSC) framework, in which a $1/\ln^2(t)$ adiabatic cooling law---inspired by the spectral statistics of the Riemann zeta zeros---is the central ansatz tying microscopic non-autonomous dynamics to time-dependent cosmological parameters.
Across eleven tests at three observational scales, the verdict is mixed at the anchor level and strongly negative on dense data.

At anchor-level resolution the framework looks plausible: Pillar~C's four-anchor $H_0$ relaxation gives $R^2 = 0.91$ and $\chi^2/{\rm dof} = 0.28$, recasting the SH0ES--Planck Hubble tension as a smooth time-dependent relaxation; Pillar~E shows that the linear-perturbation framework is internally consistent for $\gamma_{\rm eff} \in [0, 10]$; and Pillar~A reports $\Delta\chi^2 = 31.5$ improvement on a 128-absorber quasar subset.

On dense, high-precision late-universe data, the same simple ansatz fails decisively.
The cosmic chronometer compilation (Pillar~F, 32 points) reduces DSC to constant-$H_0$ behavior, giving $\chi^2/{\rm dof} = 4.0$ versus $\Lambda$CDM's $0.49$.
Pantheon$+$ (Pillar~G, 1580 SNe) rejects DSC at $\Delta\chi^2 = +617$ ($\sim 25\sigma$-equivalent).
DESI DR2 BAO (Pillar~H, 11 measurements) gives DSC $\chi^2 \approx 14000$ vs $\Lambda$CDM's $\sim 11$.
The joint 1623-constraint dataset (Pillar~J) rejects DSC at $\Delta\chi^2 = +36{,}878$, while the same data mildly prefer CPL ($w_0w_a$) over $\Lambda$CDM at $\Delta {\rm BIC} = -2.6$.
A cut-by-cut decomposition of the King~2012 quasar catalog (Pillar~K) shows that Pillar~A's apparent signal is the long-standing Keck $\alpha$-dipole: VLT-only and Keck-only subsamples give $\Gamma$ of opposite sign, none of 200 random 128-subsamples reach Pillar~A's reported $\Delta\chi^2$, and the residual signal in Pillar~A is consistent with documented wavelength-calibration systematics.

The CMB sector tells the same story.
The lattice surrogate's quantitative comparison against the CAMB Planck-2018 spectrum yields $\chi^2/{\rm dof} \approx 80$, and the lattice $D(k)$-peaks map under the geometric projection to $\ell \approx 30, 60, 90$, far from the observed $\ell \approx 220, 540, 810$; we agree with reviewer~1 that the lattice peaks are best read as standing-wave features.

\paragraph{What we retract.}
We retract: (i) any quantitative claim that DSC reproduces the Planck $C_\ell$, (ii) any claim that DSC fits late-time $H(z)$, BAO, or supernovae competitively with $\Lambda$CDM, (iii) any structural-formation interpretation of the V-shape lattice continuation past the CMB-epoch step, and (iv) the original strong reading of Pillar~A as evidence of $\alpha$-drift, in light of the Pillar~K subset decomposition.

\paragraph{What survives.}
What survives is more limited but still potentially useful: (i) a logarithmic relaxation between recombination and the present is a phenomenologically convenient description of the four-anchor Hubble tension, (ii) the framework is internally consistent at the linear-perturbation level, (iii) the differentiable JAX lattice surrogate is a methodological building block for simulation-based inference workflows that pair lattice forward models with multi-probe data, and (iv) the explicit falsifiability of Pillars F--K and the Pillar~K subset decomposition may help future workers identifying or excluding similar one-parameter relaxation models.

\paragraph{Future directions, in light of these results.}
The most informative next steps are not to defend the simple ansatz but to identify modifications that survive the late-time data: (i) generalizations of the cooling-law functional form (e.g.\ multi-scale or piecewise) that retain the recombination-to-present leverage of Pillar~C while permitting late-time shape variation needed by Pillars F--H, (ii) coupling the DSC dark-energy sector to a more flexible $w_0 w_a$ structure compatible with the mild CPL preference seen in Pillar~J, and (iii) reanalysis of the Keck-only $\alpha$-dipole subsamples after the wavelength-calibration corrections of Whitmore~\&~Murphy~(2015) and Wilczynska~\emph{et~al.}~(2020).
We do not claim the framework as currently formulated is observationally viable; we claim only that the negative results are themselves informative falsifiers, and that the differentiable lattice infrastructure built here can usefully be redirected to test successor models.
