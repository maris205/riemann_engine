\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a consolidated empirical and computational evaluation of the Discrete Symplectic Cosmology (DSC) framework, in which a $1/\ln^2(t)$ adiabatic cooling law---inspired by the spectral statistics of the Riemann zeta zeros---is the central ansatz tying microscopic non-autonomous dynamics to time-dependent cosmological parameters.
Across five tests at three observational scales, the cross-scale evidence is mixed but informative.

The framework's strongest support is at the late-time Hubble scale.
Fitting the four anchor $H_0$ measurements (SH0ES, JWST, Planck, BAO) by $H(t) = H_\infty + \beta/\ln^2(t/t_P)$ yields $R^2 = 0.91$, reduced $\chi^2 = 0.28$ (Pillar~C), recasting the SH0ES--Planck Hubble tension as a smooth time-dependent relaxation rather than a discrepancy.
At the linear-perturbation level the framework is internally consistent: five basic stability criteria pass for $\gamma_{\rm eff} \in [0, 10]$ (Pillar~E).

At the CMB+BAO scale the DSC dark-energy correction is currently \emph{unconstrained}.
A CAMB + DESI BAO Fisher analysis gives $\sigma(\gamma_{\rm eff}) \sim 1.2 \times 10^4$ (Pillar~D), so the framework is not in tension with present data but neither does it improve the fit.
The $C_\ell$ benchmark of the lattice surrogate against the CAMB Planck-2018 spectrum yields $\chi^2/{\rm dof} \approx 80$, and the lattice $D(k)$-peaks map under the geometric projection to $\ell \approx 30, 60, 90$, far from the observed $\ell \approx 220, 540, 810$; we retract any quantitative claim of reproducing the Planck $C_\ell$ and agree with reviewer~1 that the lattice peaks are best read as standing-wave features.

The quasar fine-structure-constant pillar shows a subset-dependent tension that we report rather than gloss.
A 128-absorber subset previously analyzed in the literature gives $\Delta\chi^2 = 31.5$ improvement of the DSC drift over the constant null (Pillar~A), while the full King~\emph{et al.}~(2012) 295-absorber catalog yields $\Delta\chi^2 \approx 0$ and BIC-prefers the null (Pillar~B), with the joint atomic-clock + quasar bound $|\Gamma| < 0.30$.
Whether the residual signal in the smaller subset is real or an artifact of selection requires re-examination of the absorber list and is the most concrete falsifiability target the framework currently has.

The methodological contribution is the differentiable JAX implementation of the lattice surrogate, with clean gradients through 25 St\"ormer--Verlet steps and a sphere-projection layer.
In-sample reconstruction attains $r = 0.98$ pixel correlation against Planck SMICA, but the held-out hemisphere test yields $r = -0.17$, which we treat as the primary diagnostic: with $\sim 10^6$ unknowns and $\sim 10^4$ pixels the inverse problem is severely underdetermined, and the recovered 3D field cannot be interpreted as a physical primordial reconstruction.
The differentiable lattice surrogate may still be useful as a building block for simulation-based inference workflows that pair lattice forward models with multi-probe data and explicit volumetric priors.

Other claims from earlier drafts have been revised here.
Approximate Gaussianity is reported as a consistency check rather than a non-trivial emergence.
The freeze-out scale $r_s \approx 38$ is reported as a threshold-defined effective scale; the formal horizon integral diverges logarithmically.
The continuation past the CMB-epoch step uses an Allen--Cahn toy nonlinearity rather than gravity, and is presented as phenomenological visualization rather than structure formation.
The connection to Riemann zero statistics is retained only as a working motivation for the $1/\ln^2$ ansatz, not as an established physical correspondence.

We list, in order of expected impact, the directions that we view as most worth pursuing in light of these results:
(i)~tightening the quasar drift constraint by reanalyzing the King~2012 catalog under the same selection used for the 128-absorber subset, and adding next-generation high-resolution spectroscopy;
(ii)~replacing the compressed CMB observables of Pillar~D with a full Planck likelihood and exploring whether richer lattice operators can reduce the lattice $C_\ell$ $\chi^2/{\rm dof}$;
(iii)~multi-probe inverse reconstruction combining CMB with galaxy survey data to break the projection degeneracy of the lattice inverse;
(iv)~cleaner ablations that vary one property of the integrator at a time so that any role for symplecticity in the observed statistics can be isolated.
