\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We explored whether a weakly damped St\"ormer--Verlet lattice with $1/\ln^2(t)$ adiabatic cooling---inspired by the DSC working hypothesis~\citep{WangEvolution,Wang2026} that ties cosmological time-dependence to Riemann zero spectral statistics---can reproduce statistical features of the CMB temperature anisotropies.
The experiments and the response to peer review lead us to a more conservative conclusion than our earlier framing suggested.

Quantitatively, the lattice forward model does \emph{not} reproduce the observed angular power spectrum.
Benchmarking the DSC sky maps against the CAMB Planck-2018 best-fit $D_\ell^{\rm TT}$, with a single overall amplitude as the only free parameter and a generous 20\% per-$\ell$ error budget, we obtain $\chi^2/{\rm dof} \approx 80$ over $\ell \in [30, 256]$.
Under the geometric projection $\ell \approx 2\pi R_{\rm shell}\,k$ used to make the full-sky maps, the lattice $D(k)$-peaks fall at multipoles $\ell \approx 30, 60, 90$ rather than at the observed $\ell \approx 220, 540, 810$, and the predicted peak positions drift with the projection radius and the lattice size.
We agree with reviewer 1 that the lattice peaks are best read as standing-wave features of the finite lattice and the geometric projection, not as physical analogs of baryon--photon acoustic oscillations.

Where we believe the work still contributes is methodological.
The differentiable JAX implementation enables gradient-based reconstruction through 25 St\"ormer--Verlet steps and a sphere-projection layer, attaining $r = 0.98$ in-sample pixel correlation against Planck SMICA.
The held-out hemisphere test yields $r = -0.17$, which we now treat as the primary diagnostic: with $\sim 10^6$ unknowns and $\sim 10^4$ observed pixels the inverse problem is severely underdetermined, and the recovered 3D field cannot be interpreted as a physical primordial reconstruction.
The differentiable lattice surrogate may still be useful as a building block for simulation-based inference workflows that pair lattice forward models with multi-probe data and explicit volumetric priors.

Other claims from earlier drafts are revised here.
Approximate one-point Gaussianity is consistent with the linear regime of the dynamics and is no longer presented as a non-trivial emergence.
The freeze-out scale $r_s \approx 38$ is reported as a threshold-defined effective scale, with explicit sensitivity to the threshold and grid size; the formal horizon integral diverges logarithmically and we do not interpret $r_s$ as a sound horizon.
The continuation of the lattice past the CMB-epoch step uses an Allen--Cahn toy nonlinearity rather than gravity, and is presented as a phenomenological visualization of domain coarsening rather than as a model of cosmic structure formation.
We retain the connection to Riemann zero statistics only as a working motivation for the $1/\ln^2$ ansatz.

Future work that we view as worth pursuing in light of this assessment:
(i) replacing the toy reference with a full Planck-likelihood comparison and exploring whether richer lattice operators can move $\chi^2/{\rm dof}$ closer to unity~\citep{Planck2018V};
(ii) multi-probe inverse reconstruction combining CMB with galaxy survey data (DESI~\citep{DESI2024}, Euclid) to break the projection degeneracy;
(iii) cleaner ablations that vary one property of the integrator at a time so that any role for symplecticity in the observed statistics can be isolated.
