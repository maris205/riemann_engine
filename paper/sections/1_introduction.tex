\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The temperature anisotropies of the cosmic microwave background (CMB), as mapped by the Planck satellite~\citep{Planck2018I,Planck2018VI}, encode a remarkably simple statistical portrait of the early universe: the fluctuations are nearly Gaussian~\citep{Planck2018IX}, their angular power spectrum exhibits a series of acoustic peaks at multipoles $\ell \sim 220, 540, 810, \ldots$~\citep{Planck2018V}, and the comoving sound horizon at recombination is finite~\citep{Dodelson2020}.
In the standard $\Lambda$CDM paradigm, these features arise from quantum fluctuations amplified by inflation~\citep{Planck2018X,Mukhanov2005} and processed by the baryon--photon plasma prior to decoupling~\citep{Durrer2008}.

Despite its remarkable success, $\Lambda$CDM faces persistent tensions.
The Hubble constant inferred from the CMB ($H_0 = 67.4 \pm 0.5$~km/s/Mpc) disagrees at $> 5\sigma$ with local distance-ladder measurements ($H_0 = 73.0 \pm 1.0$~km/s/Mpc), a discrepancy that has resisted resolution within the standard framework~\citep{DiValentino2021,Schoneberg2022}.
Recent baryon acoustic oscillation data from DESI provide independent evidence for dynamical dark energy ($w \neq -1$), suggesting that the cosmological ``constant'' may evolve with cosmic time~\citep{DESI2024}.
More broadly, the possibility that fundamental constants---including the fine-structure constant $\alpha$ and the gravitational coupling---vary over cosmological timescales has been a subject of sustained theoretical and observational investigation~\citep{Uzan2003,Martins2017}.

A separate line of inquiry connects the statistical properties of the Riemann zeta zeros to quantum-mechanical spectra.
The Berry--Keating conjecture~\citep{BerryKeating1999} proposes that the nontrivial zeros of $\zeta(s)$ correspond to eigenvalues of a self-adjoint operator, a hypothesis supported by random-matrix universality~\citep{Bohigas1984} and explicit Hamiltonian constructions~\citep{Bender2017}.
The density of Riemann zeros grows as $\sim 1/\ln(t)$, a scaling that has been suggested to connect to physical systems through the Hilbert--P{\'o}lya program~\citep{Schumayer2011}.
We stress that the program of identifying a physical Hamiltonian whose spectrum reproduces the Riemann zeros remains a conjecture, and the present work uses this connection only as a working motivation for a particular functional form, not as an established physical correspondence.

Inspired by these threads, the DSC framework~\citep{WangEvolution,Wang2026} explores the working hypothesis that the time-dependence of cosmological parameters might share the same $1/\ln^2(t)$ structure that arises in Riemann zero statistics, and models cosmic evolution as a non-autonomous dynamical system on a Planck-scale lattice $\Z^3 \times \N$.
Three structural assumptions---discrete spacetime, weakly damped symplectic evolution (area-preserving up to a small drag), and a Berry--Keating spectral analogy---motivate an adiabatic cooling law of the form
\begin{equation}
  \mu(n) = \mudyn + \frac{\kopt}{\ln^2(n + c_0)}\,,
  \label{eq:cooling}
\end{equation}
where $n$ is the discrete Planck time, $\mudyn$ is the asymptotic critical value, $\kopt$ is related to Feigenbaum scaling, and $c_0$ regularizes small $n$.
The $1/\ln^2$ functional form is slower than any power law and is the form picked out by the Riemann-zero analogy in Ref.~\citep{Wang2026}; we use it as a definite ansatz to study, rather than as a derived prediction.
At the cosmological-parameter level the framework yields $\Lambda(t) = \Lambda_\infty + \gamma / \ln^2(t/\tP)$ and $H(t) = \Hinfty + \beta / \ln^2(t/\tP)$ as phenomenological scaling laws.

The original DSC work validates the cooling law only through one-dimensional H\'enon map surrogates.
The question of whether spatially-extended lattice dynamics---evolving under the DSC cooling law---can reproduce the \emph{spatial} statistical features of the CMB remains open.
We emphasize that the present work is \emph{exploratory}: the lattice simulations are effective surrogate models, not direct implementations of Planck-scale quantum gravity, and we use weakly damped St\"ormer--Verlet integration with a small drag $\eta \in [0, 0.06]$, which is exactly symplectic only when $\eta = 0$.
The goal is to investigate what phenomenological features emerge from the DSC dynamical assumptions, not to claim a complete alternative to $\Lambda$CDM.

In this paper, we address these questions by implementing weakly damped St\"ormer--Verlet integrators~\citep{Hairer2006,Leimkuhler2004} on two- and three-dimensional lattices with DSC-motivated cooling.
This approach connects to the growing field of differentiable cosmological simulations~\citep{Modi2021,Li2022,Campagne2023} and simulation-based inference~\citep{Cranmer2020,Alsing2019}, as well as lattice field theory methods applied to early-universe dynamics~\citep{Figueroa2021,Caravano2022}.
Our main findings, with the framing recommended by reviewers in mind, are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Held-out hemisphere test of the inverse reconstruction (primary diagnostic).}
  The differentiable DSC physics engine permits gradient-based reconstruction of a $128^3$ primordial field from Planck sky data, achieving $r = 0.98$ pixel correlation in-sample.
  However, when half of the sky is hidden during training, the reconstructed field on the held-out hemisphere has $r = -0.17$.
  This confirms that the inverse problem is severely underdetermined ($\sim 10^6$ unknowns vs.\ $\sim 10^4$ observed pixels) and that the recovered 3D field cannot be interpreted as a physical primordial reconstruction.
  We therefore present the differentiable engine as a methodological contribution---a scalable lattice surrogate with clean gradients through 25 St\"ormer--Verlet steps and the sphere-projection layer---rather than as evidence for a particular 3D primordial state.

  \item \textbf{Quantitative comparison to the Planck angular spectrum is poor.}
  Replacing the toy analytic reference used in earlier sections with the real CAMB Planck-2018 best-fit $D_\ell^{\rm TT}$, and benchmarking the DSC sky maps against it, we find $\chi^2/{\rm dof} \approx 80$ over $\ell \in [30, 256]$ at a 20\% per-$\ell$ error budget (\cref{sec:camb}).
  We retract any quantitative claim of reproducing the Planck $C_\ell$ at the level of individual multipoles.

  \item \textbf{Lattice $k$-peaks are not the Planck multipoles.}
  The 5 oscillation peaks in $D(k)$ map to multipoles $\ell \approx 2\pi R_{\rm shell}\,k \approx 30, 60, 90, \ldots$ under the geometric projection used to generate the full-sky maps, far from the observed $\ell \approx 220, 540, 810$.
  Consistent with reviewer 1's standing-wave hypothesis, the predicted peak positions drift with both the projection radius and the lattice size.
  The lattice peaks should be read as a qualitative consequence of weakly damped wave propagation on a finite-volume lattice, not as a physical analog of baryon--photon acoustic oscillations.

  \item \textbf{Approximate one-point Gaussianity is consistent with expectations, not a non-trivial emergence.}
  In the parameter range we explore the symplectic evolution is approximately linear, so near-Gaussian one-point statistics (skewness $\approx +0.07$, excess kurtosis $\approx -0.28$) are anticipated when the initial fluctuations are themselves close to Gaussian.
  An ablation against a dissipative coupled-map-lattice baseline (kurtosis $= -1.60$) shows that strongly nonlinear dynamics destroy Gaussianity, but this comparison cannot isolate symplecticity as the unique cause because the two baselines differ in nonlinearity, conservativeness, and integration order simultaneously.

  \item \textbf{Threshold-defined effective freeze-out scale (not a physical sound horizon).}
  The $1/\ln^2(t)$ decay of the effective sound speed produces an effective freeze-out at $r_s \approx 38$ lattice units, but the formal horizon integral $\int c_{\rm eff}(t)\,dt$ diverges logarithmically and the reported scale is sensitive to the chosen amplitude threshold and to the lattice size.
  We no longer interpret this scale as an analog of the baryon--photon sound horizon.

  \item \textbf{Parameter recovery via simulation-based inference (twin experiment).}
  Bayesian optimization recovers all three lattice parameters ($c^2$, drag, $\kopt$) from the lattice power spectrum in a twin experiment (MSE $= 8.9 \times 10^{-5}$).
  This validates the differentiable engine as an inference target, but the recovered parameters describe the lattice surrogate, not the physical CMB.

  \item \textbf{Phenomenological structure-formation visualization.}
  Continuing the reconstructed field beyond the CMB epoch with an Allen--Cahn nonlinearity (a toy potential, \emph{not} gravity) produces a V-shape transition in field variance.
  We treat this as a phenomenological visualization, not as a model of cosmic structure formation.
\end{enumerate}
