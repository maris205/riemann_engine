\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What the Gaussianity finding does and does not show.}
The near-Gaussian one-point statistics of the lattice are consistent with two simple expectations rather than a non-trivial emergence.
First, the initial conditions are random Gaussian fields, and the St\"ormer--Verlet update is linear in $\phi$ in the parameter range we explore; a linear evolution of a Gaussian field is also Gaussian.
Second, each pixel of the projected sky is a sum over many lattice modes, and the central limit theorem favors Gaussianity regardless of the individual mode statistics.
The contrast with the dissipative CML baseline (kurtosis $= -1.60$) shows that strongly nonlinear dynamics destroy this Gaussianity, but the comparison cannot isolate symplecticity as the active ingredient: the two baselines differ in nonlinearity, conservation properties, and integration order simultaneously.

\paragraph{Lattice peaks vs.\ baryon--photon acoustic oscillations.}
The peaks in the lattice $D(k)$ arise from standing-wave physics on a finite-volume lattice with a time-dependent effective propagation speed.
On a sphere of radius $R$ inside the lattice they project, in the flat-sky limit, to multipoles $\ell \approx 2\pi R_{\rm shell}\,k$.
With the geometry we use this gives $\ell \approx 30, 60, 90$ for the first three lattice peaks, far from the Planck multipoles $\ell \approx 220, 540, 810$.
The peak positions in $\ell$-space drift with the projection radius and the lattice size (\cref{sec:camb}), which is the behavior expected from standing waves rather than from physical acoustic oscillations.
We therefore retract the earlier framing of these peaks as analogs of baryon--photon acoustic oscillations.

\paragraph{Status of the connection to the DSC framework.}
The simulations here implement the cooling law of Ref.~\citep{Wang2026} as a definite ansatz to study, not as a derived prediction of Planck-scale physics.
The motivation from Riemann zero spectral statistics is treated here as a working motivation for the $1/\ln^2$ functional form of the cooling law, not as an established physical correspondence.
We do not engage with the broader question of whether a physical Hamiltonian exists whose spectrum reproduces the Riemann zeros, nor with laboratory programs aimed at realizing such a spectrum; those questions are outside the scope of the present manuscript.
Within the lattice surrogate we observe that the $1/\ln^2(t)$ cooling controls the location of the threshold-defined effective freeze-out scale (\cref{sec:horizon}), but this scale is sensitive to threshold and grid parameters and we do not interpret it as an analog of the comoving sound horizon.

\paragraph{Comparison with related approaches.}
Coupled map lattices have been studied as models for spatiotemporal chaos and pattern formation~\citep{Kaneko1992}, but not in a cosmological context with adiabatic cooling.
Lattice cosmology approaches~\citep{Regge1961} discretize Einstein's equations directly, while causal set theory~\citep{Bombelli1987} replaces the manifold with a partial order.
The DSC lattice differs from both: it does not attempt to recover general relativity from the lattice, and it should not be read as an alternative formulation of cosmology.
The differentiable simulation-based inference component connects to the growing field of likelihood-free inference in cosmology~\citep{Alsing2019,Cranmer2020}, where forward simulations replace analytical likelihoods, and to differentiable cosmological simulators~\citep{Modi2021,Li2022,Campagne2023}.

\paragraph{Forward identifiability vs.\ field-level underdetermination.}
The forward twin experiment (\cref{sec:twin}) optimizes 3 global physics parameters against $\sim 75$ power-spectrum bins, an overdetermined system that recovers the parameters up to a $c^2$--$\kopt$ degeneracy direction.
The voxel-level reconstruction (\cref{sec:inverse}), in contrast, attempts to recover $\sim 10^6$ free parameters from $\sim 10^4$ sky pixels.
The high in-sample correlation $r = 0.98$ is not surprising at this ratio of unknowns to data, and the held-out hemisphere result $r = -0.17$ is the appropriate diagnostic: the inverse problem is severely underdetermined and the recovered 3D field cannot be interpreted as a physical primordial reconstruction.
We therefore present the differentiable engine---gradients flowing cleanly through 25 St\"ormer--Verlet steps and the sphere-projection layer---as a methodological building block for future multi-probe inference workflows that pair lattice forward models with volumetric priors, rather than as a tool for inferring the 3D primordial state from CMB data alone.

\paragraph{The continuation past the CMB-epoch step is a phenomenological visualization.}
The V-shape transition in field variance (\cref{sec:timeline}) is produced by a $1/\ln^2(t)$ cooling phase followed by an Allen--Cahn coarsening phase.
The Allen--Cahn nonlinearity is a generic toy potential, not a gravitational interaction, and the resulting domain morphology is generic to coarsening dynamics.
We retract the earlier suggestion of an analogy with cosmic inflation: the smoothing in our run is driven by an effective sound-speed decay rather than by a slow-roll scalar field, and the analogy is not quantitative.
The cosmic-timeline figure should be read as an illustration of the lattice's behavior under a chosen toy nonlinearity, not as a model of cosmic structure formation.
