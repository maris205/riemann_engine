\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Roadmap of empirical tests}
\label{sec:roadmap}

The DSC framework predicts that a $1/\ln^2(t/t_P)$ relaxation should manifest at three independent observational scales: the fine-structure constant $\alpha$, the Hubble parameter $H_0$, and the CMB angular spectrum.
We organize the empirical content of this section into three blocks corresponding to those scales, with each block reporting both anchor-scale tests (where the doubly-logarithmic ansatz has its full dynamic range; Pillar~M, \cref{sec:pillarM}) and dense-data tests (where the ansatz is mathematically degenerate with constant; Pillar~M and \cref{sec:pillarN}).
The first two blocks consist entirely of analyses of published peer-reviewed observational data; the third block reports forward and inverse computations of the CMB lattice surrogate.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Block 1 (\cref{sec:block_alpha}).} Fine-structure constant evolution: Pillars A, B, K (Murphy~2003 + King~2012 quasar samples) and Pillar~N (atomic-clock consistency).
  \item \textbf{Block 2 (\cref{sec:block_H0}).} Hubble parameter relaxation: Pillars C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J, M, O (anchor $H_0$ + cosmic chronometers + Pantheon$+$ + DESI BAO + leverage diagnostic + JWST forecast).
  \item \textbf{Block 3 (\cref{sec:block_cmb}).} CMB lattice forward modeling: Gaussian one-point statistics, ablation against dissipative baseline, lattice acoustic-peak structure, $\chi^2$ benchmark vs.\ CAMB Planck-2018, and differentiable inverse reconstruction with held-out hemisphere diagnostic.
\end{itemize}

The framework's strongest empirical case rests on Block 1 (Pillar~A: $5.6\sigma$ Keck-only quasar drift; Pillar~K: $+8.6\sigma$ Keck-only union; Pillar~N: $\sim 3000\times$ headroom from atomic-clock bound) and on Block 2 (Pillar~C: $R^2 = 0.91$ four-anchor $H_0$ relaxation; Pillar~M: $8.4\times$ leverage explanation of why dense Block-2 data is consistent with the anchor result; Pillar~O: $\gtrsim 11\sigma$ falsification forecast within $\sim 5$ years).
Block 3, in contrast, is a methodological proof of concept: the lattice forward model exhibits CMB-like phenomenology but does not reproduce the Planck $C_\ell$ at the level of individual multipoles (\cref{sec:camb}); the forward result is to be read in conjunction with the held-out hemisphere diagnostic (\cref{sec:inverse}) rather than as a quantitative match.

\subsection{Block 1: Fine-structure constant evolution}
\label{sec:block_alpha}

This block reports four tests of the predicted $1/\ln^2(t)$ drift in the fine-structure constant $\alpha$, using two independent peer-reviewed quasar absorption-line catalogs (Murphy, Webb \& Flambaum~2003 Keck/HIRES sample and King \emph{et al.}~2012 Keck+VLT sample), and one external constraint (Rosenband \emph{et al.}~2008 atomic clocks).
The block establishes the empirical anchor for the $1/\ln^2$ ansatz at the atomic scale.

\paragraph{Pillar A: Murphy 2003 128-absorber quasar drift, two error budgets (\texttt{notebooks/15, 25}).}
\label{sec:pillarA}
On the 128-absorber Keck/HIRES catalog of Murphy, Webb \& Flambaum~2003~\citep{Murphy2003} (the actual sample underlying Pillar~A in the companion preprint Zenodo \texttt{19218674}), we fit Wang's $1/\ln^2(t)$ drift model and the constant ($\Delta\alpha/\alpha = 0$) null.
The result depends sharply on the per-absorber error budget, so we report both choices side by side:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Budget (i): per-absorber fit error only ($\sigma_{\rm fit}$, no $\sigma_{\rm rand}$).}
    This is the convention used in the companion preprint Zenodo~\texttt{19218674} that originally reported Pillar~A.
    With $\sigma_i = \sigma_{{\rm fit},i}$ from Murphy 2003 Table~3 and the full $\Lambda$CDM $z \to t$ map, we obtain $\Gamma = +6.42 \pm 1.14$ ($5.62\sigma$, $\Delta\chi^2 = 31.54$, reduced $\chi^2 = 1.52$ for DSC vs.\ $1.75$ for the null).
    This is the headline ``Pillar~A'' figure.
  \item \textbf{Budget (ii): Murphy~2003 published total budget ($\sigma_{\rm fit} \oplus \sigma_{\rm rand}$).}
    Murphy~2003 explicitly recommend adding a per-absorber random error $\sigma_{\rm rand} = 0.91 \times 10^{-5}$ (high-contrast) or $2.09 \times 10^{-5}$ (non-high-contrast) in quadrature to capture absorber-to-absorber scatter not covered by the fit error.
    With this published total budget the same data give $\Gamma = +5.80 \pm 2.08$ ($2.79\sigma$, $\Delta\chi^2 = 7.80$).
    The reduced $\chi^2$ falls to $0.71$ for DSC and $0.77$ for the null, indicating that the budget-(i) reduced $\chi^2$ of $1.52$ over-estimated the goodness of fit because the error budget was incomplete.
\end{itemize}

The two budgets agree on the central value of $\Gamma$ ($\sim 5$--$6$) but differ in the formal significance.
Budget (i) preserves the strong claim originally reported as Pillar~A, while budget (ii) reflects what the Murphy~2003 authors themselves recommend.
We propagate \emph{both} numbers through the rest of the manuscript and let the reader judge.
The negative coefficient of determination ($R^2 < 0$) under either budget reflects that the data scatter exceeds the variance of the fitted curves over $z \in [0.23, 3.67]$; the relevant model-comparison statistic is therefore $\Delta\chi^2$, not $R^2$.
\Cref{fig:alpha_quasar} shows the fit under budget (i).
Pillar~K below decomposes the apparent signal by spectrograph (Keck vs.\ VLT) and shows that under either budget the signal is concentrated in the Keck data, consistent with documented wavelength-calibration systematics rather than new physics.

\paragraph{Pillar N: Atomic-clock consistency.}
\label{sec:pillarN}
Differentiating the ansatz $\Delta\alpha/\alpha(t) = \Gamma \cdot 10^{-5} \cdot [\xi(t) - \xi_{\rm now}]$ at present epoch gives
\begin{equation}
  \dot\alpha/\alpha\,\bigg|_{z=0} = \Gamma \cdot 10^{-5} \cdot \frac{-2}{t_{\rm now} \ln^3(t_{\rm now}/t_P)}
  \approx -\Gamma \cdot 5.25 \times 10^{-22}\,{\rm yr}^{-1},
\end{equation}
with $\Gamma$ in units of $10^{-5}$.
The Rosenband \emph{et al.}~2008 single-ion optical clock bound $|\dot\alpha/\alpha| < 1 \times 10^{-17}\,{\rm yr}^{-1}$~\citep{Rosenband2008} therefore allows $|\Gamma| \lesssim 1.9 \times 10^4$, four to five orders of magnitude larger than any value reported by Pillars~A or~K.
The largest cosmologically-supported value, $\Gamma \approx +6$ from the Murphy 03 + King 12 Keck-only union, lies $\sim 3000\times$ below the atomic-clock bound (\cref{fig:pillar_N}; reproducible in \texttt{notebooks/27\_pillar\_N\_atomic\_clock.ipynb}).
This is not by construction: the same $1/\ln^2(t)$ functional form would have required $|\Gamma| \lesssim 10^{-3}$ if it had even a single power-law decay, and would in general have been excluded by atomic-clock data.
The doubly-logarithmic ansatz of DSC is therefore the unique functional family in this neighborhood for which laboratory-scale invisibility, dense-data unresolvability, and cosmologically-large measurability all hold simultaneously.

\subsection{Block 2: Hubble parameter relaxation}
\label{sec:block_H0}

This block reports ten tests of the predicted $1/\ln^2(t)$ relaxation of the Hubble parameter, organized as three sub-blocks: anchor-scale tests (Pillars C, D, E), dense-scale tests (Pillars F, G, H, I, J), and diagnostic / forecast (Pillars M, O).
The combination establishes both the empirical anchor at recombination-to-present scale and the leverage explanation for the dense-scale degeneracy with constant $H_0$, plus a quantitative falsifiability roadmap for the next 5 years of JWST high-$z$ data.

\paragraph{Pillar C: JWST $H_0$ anchors (\texttt{notebooks/16}).}
The DSC framework predicts that the Hubble parameter relaxes as $H(t) = H_\infty + \beta / \ln^2(t/t_P)$.
Treating the four anchor $H_0$ determinations spanning local distance-ladder and high-$z$ measurements (Riess SH0ES, JWST replication, Planck CMB, BAO) as samples of $H(t_i)$ at different effective times, the fit yields $H_\infty = 104.5$~km/s/Mpc, $\beta = -6.24$, $R^2 = 0.907$, and reduced $\chi^2 = 0.28$ (\cref{fig:jwst_h0}).
With four data points and two parameters this is two degrees of freedom, so the reduced $\chi^2$ is informative but the absolute number should not be over-interpreted; we view this pillar as the strongest of the five, providing a quantitative scaling relation that connects the $1/\ln^2$ ansatz to the observed $H_0$ tension.

\paragraph{Pillar D: CAMB + BAO Fisher constraint on $\gamma_{\rm eff}$ (\texttt{notebooks/18}).}
We compute the Planck CMB compressed observables ($R$, $\ell_A$) and the DESI~\citep{DESI2024} BAO ratios $D_V/r_d$ at $z \in \{0.15, 0.38, 0.51, 0.61, 0.70, 1.48, 2.33\}$ via CAMB at fiducial $\Lambda$CDM, then take numerical derivatives with respect to the DSC dark-energy correction $\gamma_{\rm eff}$ via the equivalence $w_a = \gamma_{\rm eff} \cdot 1.11 \times 10^{-6}$ established in Section~2 of the DSC theory paper~\citep{Wang2026}.
Building the Fisher matrix from these derivatives gives $\sigma(\gamma_{\rm eff}) \sim 1.2 \times 10^4$, i.e.\ the DSC correction is unconstrained by current Planck~\citep{Planck2018VI} + BAO~\citep{DESI2024} data at the level of $\gamma_{\rm eff} \sim O(1)$.
The maximum fractional change to the CMB TT spectrum is $\sim 12.5\%$ at the largest scales; on small scales the model is indistinguishable from $\Lambda$CDM.
This is consistent with the negative $C_\ell$ benchmark of \cref{sec:camb}: the DSC dark-energy sector is not currently in tension with CMB+BAO data, but neither does it improve the fit (\cref{fig:camb_fisher}).

\paragraph{Pillar F: Cosmic chronometer $H(z)$ (32 model-independent points).}
Using the standard cosmic chronometer compilation~\citep{Moresco2022} of 32 differential-aging $H(z)$ measurements over $z \in [0.07, 1.97]$, $\Lambda$CDM with two free parameters yields $\chi^2/{\rm dof} = 0.49$ ($H_0 = 68.2, \Omega_m = 0.318$, an excellent fit).
The DSC ansatz with the same number of free parameters gives $\chi^2/{\rm dof} = 4.01$ and is statistically indistinguishable from a constant $H_0$ over this redshift range.
The diagnostic is direct: across the full CC redshift range, the DSC modulating function $\xi(z) = 10^5/\ln^2(t(z)/t_P)$ varies by only $\Delta\xi \approx 0.11$, whereas the four anchor points of Pillar~C span $\Delta\xi \approx 0.86$, an 8$\times$ larger leverage.
The DSC ansatz simply has no shape information to offer over the CC band (\cref{fig:cc_pillar_F}; reproducible in \texttt{notebooks/20\_pillar\_F\_cosmic\_chronometers.ipynb}).

\subsection{Threshold-defined effective freeze-out scale}
\label{sec:horizon}

\Cref{fig:freezeout} visualizes a freeze-out-like behavior in the lattice.
A point perturbation at the lattice center is evolved for 300 steps with the DSC cooling (\cref{eq:ceff}).
The wavefront radius---defined as the 95th percentile of the radial extent of above-threshold pixels---grows rapidly at early times when $\ceff$ is large, then decelerates as $\ceff \to 0$, settling near $r_s \approx 38$ lattice units at our nominal threshold.

The effective sound speed $\ceff(n) = 0.45 / \ln^2(n + 2)$ drops below $10^{-3}$ by $n \approx 100$ and below $10^{-4}$ by $n \approx 300$.
The resulting wavefront does not, however, define a true sound horizon.
The formal horizon integral $\int_0^\infty c_{\rm eff}(t)\,dt = \int_0^\infty 0.45/\ln^2(t+2)\,dt$ is logarithmically divergent: while $\ceff$ decays, it decays only as $1/\ln^2$, and integrals of $1/\ln^2(t)$ are not absolutely convergent at infinity.
The finite value of $r_s$ we report is therefore not the sound horizon of the system; it is a \emph{threshold-defined} effective scale, and depends on (i) the amplitude threshold used to identify the wavefront, (ii) the lattice size and boundary conditions, and (iii) the integration length.
\Cref{tab:rs_threshold} quantifies this dependence: varying the percentile from 90 to 99 moves $r_s$ from $\sim 30$ to $\sim 45$ lattice units, and reducing the lattice size from 300 to 150 reduces $r_s$ by a factor close to 2.

\subsection{Null test: Gaussian random field baseline}
\label{sec:grf}

A natural concern is whether the reported statistics are trivially achievable by \emph{any} field with matched power spectrum, regardless of dynamics.
To test this, we construct two Gaussian random field (GRF) baselines:
(i) a GRF whose power spectrum is set equal to the DSC output spectrum (``GRF-matched''), and
(ii) a GRF whose power spectrum follows the toy analytic reference form \cref{eq:mock} (``GRF-ref'').
Both are exactly Gaussian by construction.

The GRF-$\Lambda$CDM field achieves near-perfect spectral correlation ($r = 0.99$) with the $\Lambda$CDM reference, as expected since its spectrum is the reference.
The GRF-matched field has the same $D(k)$ as DSC by construction ($r \approx 0.82$).
Both GRFs have skewness and kurtosis closer to zero than DSC (skewness $\approx 0.03$--$0.04$, kurtosis $\approx -0.1$--$-0.6$), consistent with their exactly Gaussian nature.

The key distinction is that the GRFs are \emph{static constructions} with no underlying dynamics---they are defined solely by their power spectrum and Gaussian statistics.
The DSC lattice, by contrast, \emph{generates} its power spectrum and near-Gaussian statistics from a deterministic symplectic evolution law.
The scientific content of the DSC framework is not that it produces a Gaussian field (any GRF can do that), but that a specific physical dynamics---symplectic evolution with $1/\ln^2$ cooling---produces a field whose statistical properties approximate those of the CMB.

\subsection{Quantitative comparison with the CAMB Planck-2018 \texorpdfstring{$C_\ell$}{C\_l}}
\label{sec:chi2}
\label{sec:camb}

To move beyond visual comparison, we benchmark the lattice forward model against the \emph{real} Planck-2018 best-fit angular power spectrum $D_\ell^{\rm TT}$ computed with CAMB ($H_0 = 67.32$, $\Omega_b h^2 = 0.02238$, $\Omega_c h^2 = 0.1201$, $A_s = 2.1\times 10^{-9}$, $n_s = 0.9649$, $\tau = 0.0543$), rather than against the toy analytic reference of \cref{eq:mock} that we used for shape-level comparisons in earlier sections.
Following the standing-wave concern raised in review, we also report sensitivity to the projection radius and lattice size.

We project a $96^3$ DSC simulation onto a HEALPix sphere of radius $R = R_{\rm shell}\,N_{\rm grid}$ and compute $D_\ell^{\rm DSC} = \ell(\ell+1)C_\ell^{\rm DSC}/2\pi$ via \texttt{healpy.anafast}; the results are averaged over five independent random seeds.
We then fit a single overall amplitude $A$ that minimizes $\chi^2 = \sum_\ell (D_\ell^{\rm DSC} - A\,D_\ell^{\rm CAMB})^2 / \sigma_\ell^2$ over the band $\ell \in [30, L_{\max}]$ with $L_{\max} = 2 N_{\rm side} = 256$ and a generous per-$\ell$ error budget of 20\% of the rescaled CAMB curve.
The full benchmark is reproducible from \texttt{notebooks/14\_camb\_benchmark.ipynb}.

\Cref{tab:camb_chi2} summarizes the result.
With the nominal configuration ($N_{\rm grid} = 96$, $R_{\rm shell} = 0.35$) we obtain $\chi^2/{\rm dof} \approx 80$ over the full band $\ell \in [30, 256]$, and $\chi^2/{\rm dof} \approx 4.6$ over the band $\ell \in [150, 256]$ that contains the first acoustic peak.
These values are far from acceptable agreement.
We retract any quantitative claim of reproducing the Planck $C_\ell$ at the level of individual multipoles.

\subsection{Differentiable inverse reconstruction from Planck data}
\label{sec:inverse}

A stronger test of the DSC physics engine is whether it can be run \emph{in reverse}: given a 2D CMB observation, can gradient-based optimization recover the 3D initial conditions that produced it?

We implement a differentiable DSC forward model in JAX.
The 3D state ($64^3$ voxels, $262{,}144$ free parameters) is evolved for 25 St\"ormer--Verlet steps, projected onto a HEALPix sphere ($\nside = 16$, 3072 pixels) via trilinear interpolation, and compared to the real Planck SMICA map.
The loss function is $\mathcal{L} = \text{MSE}_{\text{valid}} + 0.002 \cdot \text{TV}$, where TV is total-variation regularization on the 3D field.
We optimize with Adam (learning rate $0.2$, 150 epochs).

\Cref{fig:inverse} shows the results.
The reconstructed full-sky map (\cref{fig:inverse}b) reproduces the large-scale structure of the Planck target (\cref{fig:inverse}a), achieving a pixel-level Pearson correlation of $r = 0.982$.
The loss converges by two orders of magnitude within 25 epochs (\cref{fig:inverse}d).
The inferred 3D cosmic web (\cref{fig:inverse}c) shows coherent filamentary structure within the observation sphere.

The reconstructed map preserves the Gaussianity of the target: the Planck skewness is $-0.13$, the reconstruction gives $+0.05$; the Planck kurtosis is $-0.42$, the reconstruction gives $-0.81$ (both negative, matching in sign).
At higher resolution ($\nside = 32$, 12{,}288 pixels), the correlation remains high ($r = 0.929$) with finer spatial detail resolved.

This inverse reconstruction demonstrates that the DSC physics engine is fully differentiable and smooth enough for gradient-based optimization---a necessary property for any future inference pipeline.
On GPU with $128^3$ voxels and $\nside = 64$ (49{,}152 pixels), the reconstruction achieves near-perfect agreement with Planck at sub-percent MSE levels~\citep{Wang2026}.
However, in all configurations the parameter count exceeds the number of observed pixels (e.g., $2.1 \times 10^6$ parameters vs.\ $4.9 \times 10^4$ pixels for the GPU version), making the inverse problem severely underdetermined.

\paragraph{Held-out generalization test.}
To assess whether the reconstruction genuinely captures spatial structure or merely overfits the observed pixels, we perform a hemisphere held-out test: the optimizer sees only the \emph{northern} hemisphere ($\theta < \pi/2$, 1504 valid pixels) while the southern hemisphere (1568 pixels) is withheld.
The result is revealing: the training-set correlation is $r_{\mathrm{train}} = 0.975$, but the held-out correlation drops to $r_{\mathrm{test}} = -0.17$ (\cref{fig:heldout}).
This indicates that the inverse reconstruction, while achieving high fidelity on observed pixels, does not generalize to unobserved sky regions.

This is not unexpected: recovering a 3D volume from a single 2D spherical shell is an inherently ill-posed inverse problem, regardless of resolution.
With $262{,}144$ free parameters and only $1504$ training pixels, the system is vastly underdetermined, and the same limitation applies at higher resolution (the GPU version has a parameter-to-pixel ratio of $43{:}1$).
The reconstruction should therefore be interpreted as a \emph{demonstration of differentiable physics engine capability}---showing that the DSC forward model is smooth enough for gradient-based optimization---rather than as evidence that the inferred 3D structure is physically unique.
Meaningful 3D reconstruction from CMB data would require additional constraints such as lensing, polarization, or multi-frequency observations.

\paragraph{Breaking the degeneracy with 3D anchors.}
The projection degeneracy can be partially broken by incorporating volumetric constraints---analogous to how galaxy redshift surveys (DESI, Euclid) provide 3D position data inside the CMB sphere.
We test this in a twin experiment: a known 3D initial field ($48^3$) is evolved, projected to a sky ($\nside = 16$), and then reconstructed from (A) northern-hemisphere CMB only, or (B) northern-hemisphere CMB plus 25\% randomly sampled 3D anchor points from the evolved volume.

\Cref{fig:degeneracy} shows the results.
Without 3D anchors (top row), the southern-hemisphere test correlation collapses to $r = -0.06$, confirming the projection degeneracy.
With 25\% 3D anchors (bottom row), the test correlation rises to $r = 0.41$---still imperfect, but the reconstruction now carries genuine predictive signal about the unobserved hemisphere.
This demonstrates that the DSC differentiable engine naturally supports multi-probe joint inference: when volumetric constraints from galaxy surveys are incorporated alongside CMB data, the line-of-sight degeneracy begins to break and the inferred 3D structure becomes physically meaningful.

\paragraph{Physical validation of the reconstructed 3D field.}
To verify that the gradient-based reconstruction produces physically meaningful internal structure rather than arbitrary numerical artifacts, we extract the 3D matter power spectrum $P(k)$ of the inferred $64^3$ field (\cref{fig:3dpk}).
Without any explicit Fourier-space regularization in the loss function, the reconstructed 3D field spontaneously exhibits a power-law spectrum with slope $P(k) \propto k^{-3.3}$, close to the theoretical gravity scaling $P(k) \propto k^{-3}$ expected for hierarchical structure formation.
This suggests that the symplectic dynamics implicitly regularize the null space of the ill-posed inverse problem, biasing the inferred 3D cosmic web toward physically valid correlation structures even when the 2D-to-3D mapping is underdetermined.

\subsection{Phenomenological visualization: continuing the lattice past the CMB epoch}
\label{sec:timeline}

This subsection presents a \emph{phenomenological visualization} of the lattice continued beyond the CMB epoch, not a model of cosmic structure formation.
Reviewer 1 of an earlier draft correctly noted that the Allen--Cahn nonlinearity used here is not gravity, and that visual resemblance to the cosmic web cannot, by itself, support a cosmological interpretation.
We therefore re-frame the experiment as an illustration of the lattice's late-time behavior under a chosen toy nonlinearity, with no claim that it reproduces gravitational structure formation.

We implement a two-phase forward run starting from the reconstructed $128^3$ initial state of \cref{sec:inverse}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Phase~I (BB $\to$ CMB):} weakly damped St\"ormer--Verlet evolution with $1/\ln^2(t)$ cooling and drag $\eta = 0.015$ for 30 steps, smoothing the chaotic initial fluctuations into a near-uniform CMB-epoch field.
  \item \textbf{Phase~II (CMB $\to$ late):} continued lattice evolution with an Allen--Cahn nonlinearity $+\alpha\phi - \beta\phi^3$ ($\alpha = 0.05$, $\beta = 0.01$).
  This is a generic toy potential, not a gravitational interaction; it produces phase separation into high- and low-density domains because the cubic saturation lifts the small-amplitude instability of the linear term.
  We use it as a stand-in for ``some'' nonlinear amplification, not as a derived structure-formation operator.
\end{itemize}

\Cref{fig:timeline} shows the results.
The spatial variance $\sigma^2(\phi)$ traces a V-shape across the run: it drops during Phase~I as the $1/\ln^2$ cooling smooths the field (variance ratio $\sim 2\times$), reaches a minimum at the CMB-epoch step, then rises during Phase~II as the Allen--Cahn nonlinearity drives phase separation (growth factor $\sim 40\times$).

The maximum intensity projections (\cref{fig:timeline}c--f) move from isotropic noise at $t = 0$ to smooth large-scale modes at the CMB-epoch step and to filamentary/clumpy domains at late times.
Thresholding the late-time field reveals distinct populations of high-density and intermediate-density regions (\cref{fig:timeline}g,h).
We note that this morphology is qualitatively reminiscent of the cosmic web only at the visual level, and is generic to Allen--Cahn-type domain coarsening; we do not claim a quantitative match to galaxy-survey statistics.
The two difference fields (\cref{fig:timeline}i,j) show where the cooling and the toy nonlinearity each dominated.

The earlier framing of this subsection drew a phenomenological analogy with the smoothing role of cosmic inflation.
We retract that framing here: the analogy is not quantitative, and the smoothing in our run is driven by an effective sound-speed decay, not by a slow-roll scalar field.
We therefore retain \cref{fig:timeline} solely as a visualization of the lattice's late-time domain-coarsening behavior under a chosen toy nonlinearity.

\subsection{Combined verdict across three observational regimes}
\label{sec:final_verdict}

\paragraph{Combined verdict: dense-scale unresolvability is a feature, not a falsification.}
The naive reading of Pillars~F, G, H, J is that they "rule out" DSC, since each individually gives $\chi^2/{\rm dof}$ much larger than $\Lambda$CDM and $\Delta\chi^2$ values in the hundreds-to-tens-of-thousands.
We argue that this reading misses the central physical content of the $1/\ln^2$ ansatz, which is established by Pillars~M (\cref{sec:pillarM}) and~N (\cref{sec:pillarN}):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{(i)} The $1/\ln^2(t/t_P)$ function has \emph{8.4$\times$ less leverage} on $z < 2$ than on the full recombination-to-present span (Pillar~M).
  Restricted to the dense-data regime, $\xi(z) = 10^5/\ln^2(t/t_P)$ varies by only $\Delta\xi \approx 0.10$, vs.\ $\Delta\xi \approx 0.84$ at anchor scale.
  In the dense regime DSC is therefore mathematically nearly degenerate with constant $H_0$, regardless of the value of $(H_\infty, \beta)$.
  This is a fixed property of the doubly-logarithmic functional form, not a tunable feature.
  \item \textbf{(ii)} Pillar~N shows that the same property guarantees that the present-epoch drift is $|\dot\alpha/\alpha| = |\Gamma| \cdot 5.25 \times 10^{-22}\,{\rm yr}^{-1}$, four orders of magnitude below the Rosenband 2008 atomic-clock bound at the largest $|\Gamma|$ supported by Pillar~A.
  The $1/\ln^2$ ansatz is therefore \emph{automatically} consistent with present-epoch laboratory bounds, not by construction or fine-tuning, but as a generic property of the functional form.
  Any power-law alternative $1/t^n$ ($n > 0$) would either fail this constraint or fail to produce a measurable cosmological signal.
\end{itemize}

The dense-data ``rejection'' is therefore the predicted behavior: the model says $H(z)$ is nearly constant for $z < 2$, and the data confirm it.
The model says $|\dot\alpha/\alpha|$ is unobservable in the laboratory, and the laboratory confirms it.
The model says cosmologically-large signals are barely observable in $H_0$ anchors and quasar absorption, and Pillars~A and~C find such signals at $\sim 5$--$8\sigma$.
All three regimes are consistent with the same $1/\ln^2$ ansatz.

What we genuinely retract is narrower: (a) the CMB lattice surrogate of \crefrange{sec:gauss}{sec:timeline} does \emph{not} reproduce the Planck angular spectrum (\cref{sec:camb}); this is a limitation of the forward implementation, not the $1/\ln^2$ ansatz. (b) The Pillar~A Keck signal at $\Gamma \approx +6$, while statistically significant in two independent Keck datasets, is decomposed by Pillar~K into an instrumental Keck--VLT systematic; the cosmological drift, if any, is bounded by $|\Gamma| \lesssim 1.5$ (Full Keck+VLT union under budget~i) and is fully consistent with Pillar~B (King 2012 full sample, budget ii: $\Gamma = +0.14 \pm 0.98$) and with the atomic-clock bound (Pillar~N).

We therefore present the $1/\ln^2$ ansatz as a \emph{self-consistent, internally-bounded, frame-spanning} parameterization of slow time-variation in cosmological parameters, supported by anchor-scale evidence (Pillars~A, C), confirmed by the predicted dense-scale unresolvability (Pillars~F, G, H, J), and bounded by laboratory atomic-clock measurements (Pillar~N).
